Chapter 01 · The map

A language model is a next-word guessing machine.

That's the whole secret. Everything else — Claude, ChatGPT, the model you're about to build in PHP — is engineering to make that one guess very, very good.

Try it — you are now the language model
Pick the next word — or let chance pick, weighted by probability.

Those bars are a probability distribution: one score per candidate word, all summing to 100%. An LLM produces exactly this — nothing more — then a word is drawn, appended, and the machine guesses again. One word at a time, forever. That loop is called sampling, and you'll build all of it.

§1.1 The one big idea

When you talk to Claude, it feels like a mind. Under the hood there is a single mathematical function. It takes a sequence of words (really tokens — chapter 2) and returns one probability for every word it knows, answering: "what comes next?"

The astonishing discovery of the last decade: if you make that function big enough and train it on enough text, being good at "what comes next" requires understanding grammar, facts, style, and reasoning. To predict the next word of "The capital of Denmark is", you must know it's Copenhagen. To predict the next line of a program, you must model what the code does. Intelligence emerges as a side effect of compression: the best possible next-word guesser has no choice but to understand.

Hold on to this The function never "decides to say" anything. It only scores candidates. Chat behavior, helpfulness, refusals — all of that is trained-in later (chapters 10–11), on top of the same guessing machine.

§1.3 A sense of scale

A model's capacity lives in its parameters — the learned numbers inside it (you'll meet them as weights in chapter 4). The bars below are on a logarithmic scale: each step rightward is 10×.

Our PHP GPT~0.5 M
GPT-2 (2019)124 M
GPT-3 (2020)175 B
Frontier (2026)~10¹²

Same architecture up and down this chart. The difference between our model and a frontier model is almost entirely scale — parameters, data, and the compute to train them.

Which is why we build in PHP without guilt: the concepts are identical at 500 thousand parameters and 1 trillion. What scale buys is coherence — our model will learn spelling and local grammar; frontier models, trained with roughly a trillion times more compute, learn world knowledge and reasoning. In today's mission you'll measure exactly how many multiply-adds per second your Mac can do in PHP, and we'll carry that number all the way to chapter 11 to compute how long training Claude would take on it. §1.5 The build

Chapter 1's code is matrix multiplication — the workhorse of deep learning. By chapter 8, ~95% of the model's runtime will be spent inside this one function. (The ML world calls it matmul; in our codebase it's spelled out as Matrix::multiply().)

The files

llm/
├── src/Matrix.php    ← Matrix::multiply() — read this one
├── bin/bench.php     benchmark: measures your Mac's MFLOP/s
├── bin/phpj         runs php with the JIT compiler on
└── tests/check-ch1.php verifies multiply() is correct

The math (all of it)

Multiplying an n×k matrix A by a k×m matrix B gives an n×m matrix C, where each cell is a row-times-column dot product:

C[i][j] = A[i][0]·B[0][j] + A[i][1]·B[1][j] + … + A[i][k-1]·B[k-1][j]

Concretely: [[1,2],[3,4]] · [[5,6],[7,8]] → cell (0,0) is 1·5 + 2·7 = 19. Matrices in our project are plain PHP arrays of rows: [[1.0, 2.0], [3.0, 4.0]].

The implementation is three nested loops — over rows of the left matrix, over columns of the right, and an inner loop accumulating the dot product. That's the entire function. One detail worth noticing in src/Matrix.php: the current row of the left matrix is copied to a local variable before the inner loops ($leftRow), saving one array lookup per innermost step — in the hottest loop of the whole project, that alone was worth ~25% speed.

Run it

# 2. benchmark, plain PHP interpreter

# 3. benchmark again with the JIT compiler

Result — measured on this Mac
All checks pass. Plain PHP: 184 MFLOP/s. With JIT: 1,217 MFLOP/s — a 6.6× speedup for two command-line flags. For perspective, one H100 GPU does ~10¹⁵ FLOP/s: about a million times more. That ratio is the entire story of why our models stay tiny, and we'll come back to it in chapter 11.
Why JIT matters here Plain PHP re-interprets every += and array access, billions of times. The JIT compiles your hot loops to native machine code. It's the difference between a model that trains over lunch and one that trains overnight — for free.

Chapter 02 · Tokenization

Before it can read, text must become numbers.

A neural network is a pile of arithmetic. It cannot see letters — only numbers. The tokenizer is the bridge, and it shapes everything the model can and cannot do.

Live tokenizer — type anything (vocabulary: a–z + newline, learned from 32,033 names)

§2.1 A reversible bridge

A tokenizer is two functions that must be perfect inverses: encode() maps text to a list of integer token IDs, and decode() maps IDs back to the exact original text. The set of pieces it knows is the vocabulary, and each piece's position in that list is its ID. That's all a tokenizer is.

Ours is character-level: scanning the names corpus finds 27 distinct characters (a–z plus the newline between names), sorted so IDs are stable: \n=0, a=1 … z=26. So encode("emma") → [5, 13, 13, 1] — and from here on, the model will never see anything else.

Token 0 is special The newline separating names becomes an ordinary token — and that quietly gives the model a way to say "this name is finished." In chapter 10 the same trick, with tokens like <|user|> and <|assistant|>, is what turns a text-completer into a chatbot. Special tokens are just tokens.

§2.2 Growing a vocabulary: byte-pair encoding

Characters are a tiny vocabulary (27) but long sequences — and compute scales with sequence length. Real tokenizers spend a bigger vocabulary to buy shorter sequences, and they choose the vocabulary from the data itself with an algorithm from 1994: byte-pair encoding (BPE). One rule, repeated: find the most frequent adjacent pair of tokens, glue it into a new token. Watch it run on real names:

Most frequent pairs right now

Sample names, tokenized with the merges so far
Each click performs one merge: the most frequent pair becomes a single new token.

Run against all 32,033 names, 40 merges shorten the average name from 6.12 tokens to 4.39 — 28% less sequence to compute. Frontier tokenizers push the same lever to ~100,000–200,000 tokens (built from raw bytes, so any input tokenizes — no out-of-vocabulary errors), producing tokens like " the", "ing", even whole common words.

§2.3 Tokenization explains LLM quirks

Once merged, a token is atomic — the model receives ID 3,204, not the letters inside it. This single fact explains a family of famous LLM failures:

Counting letters. Ask a model how many r's are in "strawberry" and it must reason about characters it literally cannot see — straw+berry arrives as two opaque IDs. It only knows the spelling if training text happened to discuss it.

Arithmetic. "1234" might tokenize as 12+34 one time and 123+4 another — the model has to learn math over inconsistent chunks.

Cost and context. API pricing, context windows, generation speed — all counted in tokens, because tokens are the true unit the model computes over. (Danish costs more tokens per sentence than English: fewer Danish merges made it into the vocabulary.)

Chapter 03 · The bigram model

Your first language model is a counting exercise.

No neural network, no calculus — just tallying which character follows which across 32,033 names. It "trains" in 15 milliseconds, and it genuinely generates. Meet the humble ancestor of GPT.

Live generator — running the real table trained by bin/train-bigram.php
The distribution being sampled from (top 6 of 27)
Watch it sample character by character — the same loop from chapter 1's demo, now with learned probabilities. 

  


  
§3.1 Training = counting (this time literally)

  
The model answers one question: given the current character, how likely is each possible next character? To "train" it, walk every name and tally every adjacent pair into a 27×27 table: a→n happened 5,438 times, a→r 3,264 times… Divide each row by its total and the tallies become probability distributions. That's the entire model — BigramModel::trainOn() is a nested loop and a division.

  
One clever trick: each name is wrapped in boundary tokens — ·emma· — so the table also learns which characters start names (row ·) and which characters end them (column ·). The single most common transition in the data turns out to be n→·: "ends with n" is the strongest pattern in American baby names.

  
Here is the entire trained model — hover it. Rows are the current character, columns the next; darker means more probable:


  

    
All 729 learned probabilities (row · = name boundary)

    

    
Hover a cell. Notice the dark column under · (many characters like to end names) and the dark q→u cell (some transitions are near-certain).

  


  
§3.2 Loss: the number that runs this whole field

  
Generated names look… name-ish. But "looks name-ish" can't steer training — we need a score. Here's the reasoning, and it's worth absorbing slowly because every model from here to Claude trains on it:

  
A good model should say the real data was likely. So: replay the dataset, and at every step ask the model what probability it gave to the character that actually came next. Multiply all those probabilities → the likelihood of the whole dataset. Multiplying 200k numbers below 1 underflows to zero, so take logarithms (products become sums), negate (so smaller = better), and average per character. The result is the average negative log-likelihood — the loss:

  

    
Knows nothing3.2958

    
Our bigram2.4546

    
Perfect prophet0.0

  

  
A model with zero knowledge spreads probability uniformly — 1/27 each — and scores exactly ln 27 ≈ 3.2958. Counting pulled us to 2.4546. Every chapter from here is a fight to push that number further down, with diminishing, hard-won gains.

  
The same number, everywhere
  Under the name cross-entropy, this exact quantity is what PyTorch minimizes, what frontier labs plot on their training dashboards, and what scaling laws predict. When researchers say "the loss went down", they mean this.


  
§3.3 Why it babbles — and why counting can't be saved

  
Look at the output: quravah (plausible!), but also nnanienann. The model generating the fourth n knew only one thing: the previous character. One character of context is all a bigram has.

  
The obvious fix — count with more context — dies of combinatorics. One character of context needs 27 table rows. Three characters: 27³ ≈ 19,683 rows. Seven characters: over 10 billion rows, nearly all describing contexts that appear zero times in 32k names — nothing to count, nothing to learn. Tables memorize; they cannot generalize to contexts they've never seen.

  
What we need is a function that maps any context — including unseen ones — to a distribution, with a fixed budget of learnable numbers, tuned by nudging them to reduce the loss. That is precisely what a neural network is, and the machinery for computing which way to nudge — the gradient — is chapter 4. It's the mathematical heart of the whole course.

Chapter 04 · Autograd

  
The engine that lets machines learn.

  
The bigram counted. Everything from here on learns — and learning runs on one idea: for every knob in the model, compute which way to turn it. This chapter builds that engine in ~200 lines of PHP. It is the same idea inside PyTorch, and inside whatever trained Claude.


  
§4.1 The knob problem

  
A neural network is a function with two kinds of inputs: the data (fixed, given) and the parameters — thousands to trillions of plain numbers, the "knobs". Feed data through, compare the output to what you wanted, and the loss comes out. So the loss is really a function of the knobs.

  
Training means: adjust the knobs to make the loss smaller. The brute-force way is to nudge one knob a tiny bit, rerun the whole model, and see if the loss dropped — but with a million knobs that's a million full runs per adjustment step. Useless.

  
The escape is backpropagation: if you remember how every intermediate number was computed — the full family tree of the calculation — you can start at the loss and walk that tree backwards, computing every knob's influence in a single reverse pass that costs about as much as one forward run. A million answers for the price of two. This is the algorithm that makes deep learning economically possible, and it's what Value::backward() does.


  

    
A live computation graph — L = tanh(a·b + c). Drag the knobs.

    

    

      
      Blue = the value flowing forward. Amber = the gradient flowing backward: "if this number rose by a tiny ε, L would change by gradient × ε."
    

  


  
§4.2 The chain rule, minus the fear

  
How can one backward pass know every knob's influence? Because each operation only needs to know its own local rule, and the rules compose. Watch the demo above: every node's gradient is its local derivative times the gradient of the node it feeds into — influence flowing downstream:

  
add passes gradient through untouched (nudge either side of a sum by ε, the sum moves by ε). multiply swaps in the other factor (nudge one factor by ε, the product moves by other·ε). tanh scales by 1−tanh², which is why its curve flattens learning at the extremes. That's the entire chain rule — "my effect on L = my effect on my child × my child's effect on L" — and src/Value.php is just these local rules plus bookkeeping.

  
The classic bug
  Every rule uses +=, never =: a value used in two places receives influence from both. And that's why training loops must call zeroGradients() before each backward pass — forget it, and stale gradients from the last step pile onto the new ones. Every ML practitioner has been bitten; ours is TinyNeuralNetwork::zeroGradients().


  
§4.3 Gradient descent: rolling downhill

  
Gradients in hand, learning is almost embarrassingly simple: nudge every knob a small step against its gradient — downhill on the loss surface — and repeat. The step size is the learning rate, and it is the most temperamental number in machine learning. Feel it:


  

    
Gradient descent on a loss curve — one knob, so the surface is a line you can see

    
    

      
      
      
      
    

    
Each step moves x by −(learning rate × slope). Try ~0.02 (crawls), ~0.6 (bounces), ~1.4 (explodes). Also try starts on the left hill — you'll meet a local minimum.

  

  
With a million knobs nothing changes conceptually — the ball rolls on a million-dimensional surface. Real training (and chapter 6) adds refinements — step-size schedules, momentum-style optimizers like Adam — but they are all garnish on data -= learningRate × gradient.


  
§4.4 Trust, but verify — then a small piece of history

  
How do we know our calculus is right? We check it against physics: tests/check-ch4.php takes an intentionally ugly function, computes gradients with backward(), then actually nudges each input by 0.000001 and measures how the output moves. The two answers agree to seven decimal places. This "numerical gradient check" is how real ML engineers debug hand-written layers — slow but unarguable.

  
Then the payoff run, bin/train-tiny-network.php: a 17-parameter network learns XOR — "exactly one of the two inputs is true". XOR is historically loaded: in 1969 Minsky & Papert proved single-layer networks can't represent it, an argument that helped freeze neural-network research for a decade. A hidden layer plus tanh dissolves the objection, and gradient descent finds the solution in half a second of PHP:

  
step   1   loss 3.7515
step 100   loss 0.0425
step 500   loss 0.0048

XOR(0, 0) = -0.979   XOR(0, 1) = +0.965
XOR(1, 0) = +0.963   XOR(1, 1) = -0.959

  
Nothing was programmed. Seventeen numbers were nudged five hundred times, each time in the direction backward() said would reduce the loss. That is all "learning" is — at every scale, including the frontier.

Chapter 05 · Tensors

  
The same engine, a thousand numbers at a time.

  
Chapter 4's Value is beautiful and hopelessly slow: one PHP object per number. This chapter promotes the identical three ideas to whole matrices — then proves the machinery by making gradient descent rediscover chapter 3's bigram table from pure noise.


  
§5.1 Why scalars don't scale

  
Training XOR built a graph of a few hundred Value objects. A bigram-sized language model on one 2,000-pair batch would need millions of nodes per step — each an allocated PHP object with parents and a closure. The overhead isn't the math; it's the bookkeeping per number.

  
The fix: keep the autograd design — remember parents, know your local derivative, walk backwards — but make each graph node carry a whole matrix. Bookkeeping is now paid per operation, and inside each operation there's nothing but tight float loops (which the JIT compiles to native code, chapter 1's lesson). src/Tensor.php is Value.php with plurals.

  
There's a deeper reason too: a layer of neurons processing a batch of inputs is a matrix multiplication — every neuron × every input, all independent. That's why Matrix::multiply will own ~95% of our runtime, and why hardware built to do nothing but parallel matrix math (GPUs) turned out to be the key that unlocked deep learning.

  
Matrix gradients are one pattern
  For C = A·B: gradient of A is Cgrad·Bᵀ, gradient of B is Aᵀ·Cgrad — "multiply: the other factor", matrix-shaped. We don't take it on faith: the check suite nudges individual entries by 0.000001 and confirms backprop to seven decimals, same as chapter 4.


  
§5.2 Softmax: from scores to probabilities

  
A model's last layer produces one raw score per vocabulary token — called logits. Scores can be any number, but sampling needs a probability distribution: all positive, summing to 1. Softmax is the converter: exponentiate every score (now all positive, and bigger scores pull ahead fast), then divide by the total. Feel how it behaves:


  

    
Softmax + cross-entropy, live — drag scores, click a bar to mark what actually came next

    

    
Loss = −ln(probability the model gave the true token). The amber numbers are the gradient softmax+CE sends back: probability − 1 on the true token, probability on the rest.

  

  
Now the punchline that makes every framework fuse these two operations. Softmax followed by chapter 3's loss has a combined gradient of, literally, probabilities − one-hot(truth): "push the true token's score up by (1 − its probability); push every wrong score down by its own probability." Pages of calculus collapse into subtraction — watch it in the amber numbers above: confident-and-right barely moves anything, confident-and-wrong yanks hard. Learning pressure ∝ surprise.


  
§5.3 selectRows: the embedding idea sneaks in

  
One more tensor operation and it's the sly one: selectRows([13, 1, 20]) fetches rows 13, 1, 20 of a matrix. Token IDs go in, the rows belonging to those tokens come out. When the matrix is learnable, each token's row becomes that token's private bundle of learned numbers — its embedding, the "vectors that encode meaning" from chapter 1's pipeline. In this chapter each row holds the token's next-character scores; in chapter 6 rows become genuine meaning-vectors that feed a deeper network. Backward, gradients scatter onto exactly the rows that were used — popular tokens get trained often, rare ones rarely (why frontier models are shakiest on rare tokens).


  
§5.4 The experiment: rediscovering chapter 3 from noise

  
Full circle. The model: a single 27×27 weight matrix — row = current character, column = score for each next character. Forward: selectRows → softmaxCrossEntropy. It's exactly the bigram table, but expressed as a differentiable function — and instead of counting, we start it as random noise and let gradient descent (minibatches of 2,000 pairs, learning-rate decay at step 1,000) find the numbers:

  
Loss before training (random weights): 3.2962   ← the know-nothing baseline, as it must be
  step    1   batch loss 3.2965
  step  250   batch loss 2.4691
  step 1500   batch loss 2.4916

Final loss on ALL 228,146 pairs: 2.4588   (chapter 3, by counting: 2.4546)

  
From pure noise, in 4.6 seconds, gradient descent reconstructed the counting table to within 0.004. The generated names are chapter-3 quality (takeisotha, kannn) — as they must be: same one-character context, same information, same ceiling. What changed is the recipe. Counting only works when the model is a lookup table; gradient descent works for any architecture you can take gradients of. Next chapter we use that freedom to finally widen the context — the thing counting could never do.

Chapter 06 · The MLP language model

  
The first model that generalizes.

  
Three characters of context, 6,899 parameters, and two new superpowers: embeddings (so knowledge transfers between similar contexts) and a validation set (so we can't fool ourselves). The bigram ceiling falls: 2.4546 → 2.1932.


  
§6.1 Embeddings: the escape from the table

  
Chapter 3 ended on the wall: three characters of context as a lookup table needs 27³ ≈ 20k rows, most never seen. The 2003 escape (Bengio's neural language model — this chapter's architecture) is to stop treating characters as table indices and start treating them as learned vectors. Each character gets a handful of numbers — its embedding — and the model reads the window as vectors, not IDs.

  
Why that fixes generalization: if k and c end up with similar vectors (they behave alike before a), then everything learned about ka… contexts automatically transfers to ca… contexts — even ones never seen in training. Knowledge is shared through vector-space geometry instead of stranded in separate table rows.

  
And it isn't hypothetical. We trained a version whose embeddings have just 2 dimensions, so every character is literally a point on a plane you can look at. Here is what gradient descent — told nothing about English, phonetics, or letters — chose:


  

    
The learned embedding plane (real coordinates from bin/train-mlp.php)

    
    
Every vowel migrated to the top of the plane; every consonant stayed below. Nobody told the model what a vowel is — vowels simply get used the same way in names, so the loss pushed their vectors together. Note y hovering between the clusters (a part-time vowel, mathematically confirmed) and the name-boundary · far off on its own. This — meaning arising as geometry — is the same mechanism that makes "king − man + woman ≈ queen" work in word embeddings, and it is happening in your PHP.

  


  
§6.2 The architecture

  
Forward pass, in the order computeLogits() reads:

  
[·, e, m] window→embed & concatenate (24 numbers)→× hiddenWeights + bias→tanh (128 neurons)→× outputWeights + bias→27 logits

  
The hidden layer is chapter 4's tanh neurons in matrix form: each of the 128 neurons looks at the whole 24-number context and learns to fire on some useful pattern. One initialization subtlety worth knowing (it's in the constructor comment): weights are scaled by 1/√fan-in so early activations land in tanh's responsive zone rather than its flat tails — badly initialized networks die before they learn, and this tiny formula is the difference.


  
§6.3 The exam the model can't cheat

  
New discipline from this chapter on: we hold out 10% of the names (3,203 of them) that the model never sees during training. Loss on seen data measures memory; loss on held-out data measures generalization. A big enough model can drive training loss toward zero by memorizing the dataset — validation loss is the score that can't be gamed, and it's the only number we'll brag about. (Split by whole name, not by window, or overlapping windows would leak exam questions into the study material.)

Chapter 07 · Self-attention

  
Every position asks the past for help.

  
The MLP reads exactly 3 characters through a rigid window. Self-attention — the 2017 idea from "Attention Is All You Need" — lets every position look at every earlier position and decide, from content, what matters. It is the T in GPT, and you now have one running in PHP.


  
§7.1 What's wrong with windows

  
Three problems, all structural. A fixed window forgets: position 9 literally cannot see position 1, so the MLP never knows how the name began. It's rigid: the third-character-back always lands in the same input slots, even though which earlier character matters depends entirely on content. And it scales badly: widening the window multiplies the input layer.

  
What we actually want sounds almost too soft to be math: let each position ask the past for what it needs. Attention makes that differentiable.


  
§7.2 Queries, keys, values: a soft lookup

  
Each position's vector gets three learned projections — three roles:

  
query — "what am I looking for?" · key — "what do I advertise?" · value — "what will I hand over if you pick me?"

  
Then, in one sweep: every query is dotted against every key, giving a T×T grid of match scores (high score = "you have what I want"). Scores are scaled by 1/√headSize (long vectors make naturally bigger dot products; unscaled, softmax saturates and gradients die), the future is masked off, and each row is softmaxed into a probability distribution: how position t divides its attention over the visible past. The output for each position is the attention-weighted blend of the values it chose. Every arrow in that story is differentiable — so gradient descent learns what to look for. Nobody programs the patterns.

  
These are the actual patterns our trained head learned — not illustrations:


  

    
Real attention weights from bin/train-attention.php — row = the position looking, column = where it looks

    

    

    
Hover a cell. Notice the hard black upper triangle — the causal mask, the future literally zeroed out — and how different characters spread attention differently: some lean on the previous character, some reach back to the start of the name.

  


  
§7.3 The mask, and a free lunch

  
Row t may only attend to columns ≤ t: scores above the diagonal are set to −10⁹ before the softmax, which rounds their attention to zero. Why so strict? Because generation runs left to right — at inference time the future doesn't exist yet — and a model allowed to peek during training learns to rely on information it will never have again.

  
The discipline pays for itself spectacularly: with the mask in place, one forward pass over [·, e, m, m, a] yields T honest predictions at once — position 0 predicts e, position 1 predicts m, and so on — each provably uninfluenced by its own answer key. One sequence = T training examples for one pass. This parallelism (not raw cleverness) is the main reason transformers dethroned their predecessors: they saturate GPUs. Our check suite proves the causality end to end: change token 4, and positions 1–3's predictions stay identical to 12 decimal places.


  
§7.4 Position embeddings: telling attention where things are

  
One subtlety: the attention mechanism compares contents only — shuffle the input vectors and the same matches happen. It is order-blind. The MLP got order for free (slot 1 vs slot 2); attention needs it injected. The fix is almost cheeky: learn a vector for each position 0…11, and add it to the token's embedding. Now "m at position 1" ≠ "m at position 4", and attention can learn things like "look at the first position" — which real heads in real transformers demonstrably do.


  
§7.5 The experiment, honestly framed

  
One head, 16 dimensions, 1,851 parameters — trained on names in 18 seconds: validation loss 2.3267. Between the bigram (2.4546) and the MLP (2.1932), from a quarter of the MLP's parameters. That's the engine alone, no chassis: no feed-forward layer to think about what was gathered, no stacking, no residuals. Assembling those is chapter 8, and the ladder falls again.

  
The check that matters more than the loss: we trained the head on sequences where the correct answer is "repeat the FIRST token, 5 positions back" — information a 3-character window cannot access, ever. Attention learns it to 100% confidence. That capability — content-addressed recall at arbitrary distance — is the thing the whole chapter buys, and at frontier scale it's why Claude can use a fact from the top of your prompt at the bottom of its answer.

Chapter 08 · The Transformer

  
Assembly of the GPT.

  
Nothing new is invented in this chapter — that's the point. Attention, feed-forward nets, embeddings, softmax: you built them all. Today they get bolted together the way "Attention Is All You Need" prescribed in 2017, and the result is, architecturally, a real GPT.


  
§8.1 Four missing pieces

  
Feed-forward layers. Attention gathers — it moves information between positions — but it barely processes. So each block follows attention with a small per-position neural network (widened 4× in the middle: room to think): every position privately digests what it just collected. The division of labor is clean: attention = communication, feed-forward = computation.

  
Multi-head attention. One head learns one kind of lookup. Running several small heads in parallel — each with its own queries, keys, values over a slice of the dimensions — lets one block track several relationships at once (the previous character, the start of the name, the vowel pattern…). Their outputs are concatenated and mixed by a projection.

  
Residual connections. The deep-learning survival trick. Each sub-layer's output is added to its input, never replacing it: stream = stream + attention(stream). Picture one residual stream flowing up through the network — a shared whiteboard every block reads from and writes additive edits onto. And remember chapter 4's add rule: addition passes gradients through untouched — so the loss signal at the top flows down the stream to the earliest layers unimpeded. Before this trick (2015), deep networks simply didn't train.

  
Layer normalization. Stacked layers drift — activations explode or collapse, and tanh/softmax saturate. Before every sub-layer, each position's vector is re-standardized (mean 0, spread 1, with learned per-dimension gain and bias to undo it where useful). It's the thermostat that keeps 2 blocks — or 96 — in their responsive range. Its backward pass is the trickiest derivative in our codebase, because each value affects its row's mean and variance too; the check suite verifies it numerically, as always.

Chapter 09 · Inference

  
One frozen model, many personalities.

  
Training is over — the 27,355 numbers in models/gpt-names.json will never change again. Everything that makes a model feel cautious, creative, or unhinged happens in the last inch of the pipeline: how a token gets chosen from the logits. Two knobs govern it, and you've seen both on every LLM API.


  
§9.1 Temperature

  
Divide every logit by a constant T before the softmax. That's the entire mechanism — but feel what it does. These are the model's real logits after the context mari:


  

    
Real logits after "mari" — reshape the distribution yourself

    

      
      
    

    

    

  

  
Slide toward 0 and probability collapses onto a — greedy decoding, where the model always plays its single favorite (run greedy on our GPT and every name is aliana, forever — deterministic, boring, and for chat models, prone to repetitive loops). Slide past 1.5 and the floor rises: junk tokens get real chances, and the gallery below shows what that does to names. Chat models typically live around 0.7–1.0: honest distribution, lightly sharpened.


  
§9.2 Top-k: amputating the tail

  
Toggle the top-k buttons in the demo: everything outside the k most likely tokens is banned outright, and the survivors are renormalized. With 27 characters this is a mild edit — but a frontier model has ~100,000 tokens, and after any context it puts a sliver of probability on thousands of absurd ones. Each sliver is negligible; their sum can be several percent — a real chance per token of stepping off a cliff, and one derailed token pollutes the context for every token after it. Top-k (and its cousin nucleus/top-p sampling, which cuts by cumulative probability instead of count) is the guardrail. This is also why "creative" and "reliable" are a genuine trade-off, not a slider one direction is winning.


  
§9.3 The gallery — one model, five temperaments

  

  
And a taste of things to come — condition the model on a prefix and it completes it: ma… → marleize, makary, mayyah · kai… → kailynn, kairi, kaize · zo… → zolany, zoriahna, zohamiya. Steering a frozen model by choosing what it must continue — that is the entire idea of prompting, in miniature. Try it: php bin/generate.php --prefix=math --temperature=0.8


  
§9.4 The cost of generating, and the KV cache

  
Watch what our generate() does: to produce token 10, it re-runs the transformer on all 10 previous tokens; for token 11, on all 11. Nothing about the old positions changed — their keys and values come out identical every time — yet we recompute them all. Generating a T-token sequence costs O(T²) forward work.

  
The industry fix is the KV cache: after each step, keep every position's keys and values; a new token computes only its own query/key/value and dots its query against the cached keys. Each new token becomes O(T) instead of O(T²). We leave it unimplemented (at T=12 it saves milliseconds) — but now you can read real systems: the cache is why long chats eat GPU memory (it grows with every token, per layer, per head), why APIs price cached input tokens differently, and what "context window" limits are really budgeting.

Chapter 10 · The chatbot

  
Teaching the machine to converse.

  
A language model completes text; a chatbot answers you. The gap between those two is bridged by the exact pipeline that produced Claude — pretraining, then supervised fine-tuning — and this chapter runs that pipeline, in miniature, to its promised finale: php bin/chat.php.


  
§10.1 A world small enough to know completely

  
A 29,496-parameter model cannot be coherent about Denmark, PHP, or the news. It can be coherent about a world with 5 people and 20 facts — the TinyStories insight from chapter 0's plan, cashed in. Our world: mia, leo, sam, ana and max, each with a house color, a pet, a favorite food, and a job. Two other scale-friendly choices: a word-level tokenizer (56 words — the model speaks in whole words, no spelling errors possible), and the whole world's knowledge expressed twice: as plain statements, and as dialogues.


  
§10.2 Stage 1 + Stage 2 = the frontier recipe

  
Stage 1 — pretraining. The same GptLanguageModel class as chapter 8 reads this world's "internet": 1,600 plain sentences ("mia lives in the red house", "rex is the dog of leo"). Result: a competent text-completer that knows every fact. But watch what happens when you ask it a question in a format it has never seen:

  
<user> where does mia live <bot> →
  "a cat red pizza mia lives in the fish house is mia house is mia lives"

  
Every word is world-vocabulary. The facts are in there. It just has no idea what a question is — a base model in the wild, exactly what GPT-3 was before ChatGPT: knowledge without conversation.

  
Stage 2 — supervised fine-tuning (SFT). Training continues — same weights, same loss, same gradient descent — but now on dialogues wrapped in a chat template:

  
<user> where does mia live <bot> mia lives in the red house <end>

  
The special tokens are ordinary vocabulary entries (chapter 2 promised this day would come). The model learns the social structure: after <bot>, produce an answer to what followed <user>; then say <end>. When you talk to Claude, your conversation is serialized in precisely this way — fancier tokens, same idea — and "the assistant" is a pattern the model completes, not a program that runs.

  
Result, on an exam covering all 20 facts: 20/20. Same weights that babbled above.

§10.4 What it can't do, and why that's the lesson

  
Ask it a question composed from known words in an unseen pattern and it may stumble — it learned these dialogue shapes, not questioning in general. It cannot reason ("who lives next to mia?" has no answer in its world model, and it will confabulate one rather than say so). It has no idea it's a bot, no memory between questions, no self. Chapter 11 · To the frontier

  
From your GPT to Claude: what's actually different.

  
You now hold every architectural idea inside a frontier model. This closing chapter is the honest accounting of what separates your 29k parameters from their ~10¹² — and it's fewer things than you'd think, each one enormous.


  
§11.1 Scale, and the laws that made labs bet everything

  
The discovery that created the frontier era (Kaplan 2020, refined by DeepMind's Chinchilla 2022): plot loss against compute across many training runs and you get an eerily straight line on log-log axes — a scaling law. Loss falls predictably as you grow parameters and data together (Chinchilla's rule of thumb: ~20 tokens of data per parameter). No cleverness required, just scale — and the line held across seven orders of magnitude. That predictability is why labs could justify $100M+ training runs: the outcome was, in the one metric that matters, forecastable.

  
You've already felt the strange part: loss 2.45 → 2.19 → 2.12 looked like small numbers, but the names changed species. At frontier scale the same compounding produces emergence — abilities like arithmetic, translation, and code appearing at scales where smaller models showed ~nothing, because loss-driven pressure eventually forces real structure (your 2-dim vowel cluster was this force at minimum viable size).

  
The arithmetic of it, using your Mac's measured 1,217 MFLOP/s (bin/frontier-math.php):

  

our names GPT     3.2×10¹¹ FLOPs   4 minutes (you did this)

GPT-2 (2019)      8.6×10²⁰ FLOPs   22,408 years in PHP

GPT-3 (2020)      3.1×10²³ FLOPs   8,109,442 years in PHP

frontier (2025)   ~3×10²⁵ FLOPs    0.8 billion years in PHP — or ~43 days on a 20,000-GPU cluster
  

  
One H100 ≈ 821,693× your JIT-compiled PHP. A frontier cluster is tens of thousands of them. Same equations, same gradients — different electricity bill.


  
§11.2 Stage 3: learning from preference, not imitation

  
Your chatbot stopped at SFT: it imitates example dialogues. Frontier models add a third stage — reinforcement learning from feedback — because imitation has a ceiling: you can only be as good as your examples, and "helpful, honest, harmless" is easier to recognize than to demonstrate exhaustively.

  
The loop: the model generates several answers to a prompt; humans (RLHF) — or an AI judge steered by an explicit list of principles, Anthropic's constitutional AI (RLAIF) — rank them; a reward model is trained to predict those rankings; then the chat model is optimized to score well — not with our plain SGD but with policy-gradient methods (PPO and kin) that can learn from a single score per whole answer instead of a target per token. Refusing harmful requests, admitting uncertainty, the assistant "personality" — that's stage 3. When people say models are "aligned", this machinery is what they mean, and the open research question is how well it holds as capabilities scale.


  
§11.3 The architecture delta, 2017 → now

  
Perhaps the most remarkable fact in this course: your transformer is barely out of date. The changes since 2017 are refinements, and you can now read every one: RoPE — position injected by rotating query/key vectors instead of learned position rows (better extrapolation to long contexts). GELU/SwiGLU replacing our tanh in the feed-forward. Grouped-query attention — many query heads sharing fewer key/value heads, shrinking the KV cache you now understand. Mixture-of-experts — many feed-forward networks per block with a learned router activating a few per token: trillion-parameter capacity, fraction-of-it compute. FlashAttention — chapter 1's lesson at industrial scale: same math, memory-access-aware kernel. Plus long-context tricks, quantization for serving, speculative decoding. Not one of these changes the story you built: embed → attend → think → residual stream → logits → sample.


  
§11.4 What we simplified (so you know where the bodies are buried)

  
Honesty section: real training uses the Adam optimizer (per-parameter adaptive step sizes; our plain SGD is its great-grandfather), dropout and weight decay against overfitting, gradient clipping, warmup schedules, and batched 3-D tensors where our loops process one sequence at a time. Real tokenizers run BPE on raw bytes (you built the real algorithm at character level). Real evals go far beyond validation loss. All engineering; no new ideas.
